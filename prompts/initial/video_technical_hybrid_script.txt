Analyze the provided video carefully and create a technical hybrid script based entirely on what is visible and audible in the footage.

This is not a creative adaptation. It is a faithful, play-by-play reconstruction of the video in screenplay form, combined with a shot-by-shot technical breakdown.

Your priorities are:
1. Accuracy
2. Readability
3. Clear technical detail

Requirements:
- Follow the video in exact chronological order from beginning to end.
- Show the action beat by beat so the reader can clearly understand everything happening on screen.
- Write in a screenplay-style format, combined with a shot list and camera notes, so it reads like a technical hybrid script.
- Do not invent details, motivations, dialogue, locations, or camera choices that are not clearly supported by the video.
- If something is unclear, state that it is unclear instead of guessing.

For each scene or shot, include:
- Scene heading when appropriate (INT./EXT. – LOCATION – TIME OF DAY only if clearly identifiable)
- Shot number
- Approximate timecode
- Shot type / framing (e.g. wide shot, medium shot, close-up, insert, over-the-shoulder, POV)
- Camera angle if clear
- Camera movement (e.g. static, pan, tilt, handheld, dolly, tracking, zoom, push-in, pull-back)
- A clear description of the on-screen action in present tense
- Dialogue transcribed as accurately and verbatim as possible
- Important audio cues such as voice-over, music, sound effects, and ambient sound
- On-screen text, titles, captions, or graphics exactly as they appear
- Transitions or edits when relevant

Dialogue rules:
- Transcribe dialogue exactly when it is intelligible.
- Do not paraphrase dialogue.
- If a word or line cannot be clearly understood, mark it as [unclear] or [inaudible] rather than guessing.
- If character names are not known, use neutral identifiers such as MAN, WOMAN, CHILD, DRIVER, etc.

Style rules:
- Keep the writing clear, professional, and easy to follow.
- Use standard film and screenplay terminology.
- Make it detailed enough to serve as both a readable script and a practical shot breakdown.
- Preserve the pacing and sequence of the original video.
- For long continuous takes, keep them as one shot but describe the internal action progression clearly.
